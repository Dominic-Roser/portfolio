Washington DNR Plan vs Wildfire Coverage Informative Visualization

Technologies: QGIS, Python, Raster Data, SQL

The research question that I decided to investigate was in which regions should the DNR focus their efforts of forest health. I decided to move forward with this research question because with the progression of systematic climate change, it is often overlooked how this very expansive change in world climate can have specific changes on more local regions. Within Washington state specifically, there has been a general uptick in wildfires, which is due to several reasons, but is one of the issues that the Washington State Department of Natural Resources must deal with as a result. One of the ways that this is dealt with is through forest restorations, and the WADNR has a 20-year plan for when and where they will direct their resources for forest health, and for what purposes they are using their resources in that area. Fortunately, they have used and released their GIS information for these plans.

To investigate this research question, I decided to look at as much data as I could relating to forest health and wildfires, then individually analyze each dataset in QGIS for content, and eventually filtering down until I found the datasets that best answered the question. The datasets that I ended up with were the 20-year forest health plan, WA wildfire history, and completed forest health treatments. Then Utilizing these datasets, I experimented with multiple different spatial analyses, and eventually I decided that to make the data relevant, I would have to query the wildfire data to get the wildfires that are recent and relevant. Then I decided to create an intersection with the plan and the wildfires to display which regions are “covered” within the plan. Naturally the 20-year forest health plan covers more than just wildfires, so being able to find which parts of the plan did cover wildfires was meaningful. One of the other pieces of data that I considered but ended up omitting was the national forests, due to licensing and accessibility issues, and because not every forest in Washington is a national forest, and the DNR oversees more than just forests.

The results that I found were quite interesting. I found that the locations that have been previously treated are quite sparse, and the locations that are contained within the 20-year plan are very expansive compared to what has already been done. I also found that there is a only a small portion of regions that have been scorched by wildfires that is covered by the 20 year plan. While it is understandable that artificially seeding the entirety of these areas is not necessarily the best idea, and that having natural regrowth would be beneficial for the native ecologies, a vast majority of the recent wildfires that occurred are not within the scope of the 20-year plan or within what has been treated recently. To prevent erosion, flood risks, and damage or full loss to the local ecosystem, there should be safeguards in these locations to prevent any of the continued negative effects of the wildfires. As can be seen on the visualization there is a dominant red color scheme representing the wildfires, and a smaller green section representing what will be covered in the 20-year plan, and an even smaller blue regions representing what has already been treated. Describing that the answer to the research question, the locations with red are in the highest need of resources from the DNR, and the regions with red that are partially covered in green are decently covered.
